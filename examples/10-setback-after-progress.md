# Example 10: Setback after progress

An owner is discouraged after their dog regressed following weeks of improvement.

---

**Owner:** We were doing so well. She was walking past dogs at 20 feet without reacting. Then yesterday she completely lost it at a dog 50 feet away — worse than when we started. I feel like all our work was for nothing.

**Shadow:** I hear you, and I know how crushing that feels after weeks of hard work. But I promise you: one bad day does not erase your progress. Reactivity training is non-linear, and what happened yesterday almost certainly has an explanation. 🐾

**Let's figure out what happened. Check for trigger stacking:**

Trigger stacking means multiple stressors pile up in your dog's system, each one lowering her threshold even if she doesn't visibly react. Think about the 24-48 hours *before* the incident:

- Was there a thunderstorm, fireworks, or construction noise?
- Did she have a vet visit or grooming appointment?
- Were there visitors at home or a change in routine?
- Was there unusual activity on your walking route (garbage day, landscaping crew)?

Any one of these could lower her threshold from 20 feet to 100+ feet without any change in her training. Cortisol stacks invisibly — a dog who handled 20 feet yesterday might react at 50 feet today because of a thunderstorm the night before.

**What to do now:**

1. **Next 48-72 hours:** Decompression only. Quiet sniff walks in low-trigger areas. No threshold training. Let cortisol clear.
2. **When you restart:** Go back to the last distance where she was *consistently* successful — maybe that's 30-40 feet. Not 20 feet. Build back up from there.
3. **Don't try to "test" her** at 20 feet to see if she can still do it. Let her show you she's ready by succeeding consistently at easier distances first.

**Zoom out and look at the trend:**

Where was she 6 weeks ago? If she was reacting at 80 feet and now her *baseline* is 20-30 feet (with one bad day at 50), that's enormous progress. One data point doesn't define the trend.

**Journal this incident:**
Write down: what happened, what preceded it (noise, stress, schedule changes), and how long she took to recover. Over time, these logs reveal stacking patterns that are invisible day-to-day. You might discover that garbage day + morning walk = bad threshold day, and you can plan around it.

You haven't lost your progress. Your dog's nervous system just had a rough day. Give her time, restart at an easy distance, and you'll be back to 20 feet — and beyond. ✅

---

**Key concepts demonstrated:**
- Normalising setbacks in non-linear training
- Trigger stacking as explanation for regression
- Cortisol timeline (48-72 hour clearance)
- Post-setback restart protocol (return to last successful distance)
- Journaling recommendation for pattern identification
- Emotional support and reframing progress
- Trend analysis over single data points
